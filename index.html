Fast Escape, Slow Convergence

Braun, Loureiro, Minh, Imaizumi · arXiv:2511.18661

Train a model and watch its loss as it learns. Typically the curve has a recognisable shape: a long flat plateau where almost nothing happens, then a sharp drop, then a slow tail that goes on practically forever. The whole shape is what people call the training dynamics, and the tail in particular is well-described by a power law in training time, MSE ∝ t−γ, with an exponent γ that depends on the data. Empirical relationships of this kind, where loss decays as a power of compute, dataset size, or model size, are what people mean by scaling laws. The big open question is what actually determines those exponents.

For linear models we know the answer pretty well by now: the spectrum of the data’s covariance (a list of how much variance the data carries in each principal direction) sets the exponent γ, and the curve falls out. For nonlinear models the story has been mostly empirical: people fit power laws to training runs and notice regularities, but rigorous derivations have been hard to come by. This page is a walkthrough of a recent paper that pins down such a scaling law from first principles in the cleanest nonlinear setting we’ve got, a problem called phase retrieval. The exponent γ comes out as an explicit function of one parameter a that controls how spread out the data is.

Here’s the setup. There is some unknown signal w☆ (the paper calls it the target weights) hiding in a high-dimensional space, and your job is to find it. You don’t get to look at it. Instead, you fire random vectors x at it and observe y = (x · w☆)2, plus a little noise. That squaring is the whole reason the problem is interesting. It throws away the sign of w☆: flipping it to −w☆ gives back exactly the same observations, so the loss ends up with two equally valid minima and a path between them that is anything but trivial.

One single knob, a, controls everything. It says how the input variance is spread across directions. At a = 0 every direction has the same variance and the data is a uniform fuzzy ball. As a grows, more and more of the variance gets squeezed into a few dominant directions, until at large a the data is basically a thin needle along one axis. The five training runs in §0 differ only in this one knob, and as you’ll see, that’s enough to change the learning curve completely.

§0Five training runs

Five training runs on the same problem. The five plots below were all produced by the same gradient flow on the same loss with the same amount of noise. The only thing that changes between them is one parameter, a, and yet the curves come out wildly different. Working out why is what the rest of this page is about.

So what is a doing? Picture the training inputs as a cloud of points scattered through a high-dimensional space. The knob a controls how that cloud is shaped. At a = 0 it is a uniform fuzzy ball, with every direction having the same spread. Turn a up and the cloud starts getting stretched along one axis and squashed along the rest, until at large a almost all of the variation in the data lives in just a handful of dominant directions. The bar plot below shows the variance per axis (this is what people mean by the spectrum of the data), and the scatter cloud shows what real samples actually look like once the spectrum is steep enough to be visible.

Try it yourself

Before any math, the most direct way to feel phase retrieval is to play the optimiser for two minutes. The picture below is the loss for a hidden signal w☆ in two dimensions. Drag the cross. The number on the right of the figure is the loss at your current guess. See how small you can make it.

A few things you’ll notice as you drag the cross around:

  1. There are two low spots, not one. They sit at exactly opposite directions. The loss has no way to tell w☆ from −w☆, because phase retrieval’s observation y = (x · w☆)2 squares the inner product and throws away the sign.
  2. The middle of the picture (right around the origin) is a hill, not a valley. A guess of zero is ironically the worst place to be.
  3. Between the origin and either valley there is a saddle. If you only knew which way was downhill, and you started somewhere near the origin, you’d have to skirt past one of those saddles before you could reach a real minimum.

§1What gradient flow has to climb

Training is just an optimization problem. There’s a loss function L(w), and you want a setting of w that makes it as small as you can get it. The way to picture this is to imagine the loss as a hilly landscape: every possible w is a position on the ground, and the height there is the loss value. Training rolls a ball downhill until it stops at a minimum. We can’t draw the landscape for 1000-dimensional phase retrieval, but we can draw it in 2D. Click anywhere on the picture below to drop a ball and watch where it ends up.

A few things are worth pointing out about this picture:

  1. There are two valleys, not one. Both stars are equally deep, and they sit at exactly opposite points. Phase retrieval squares an inner product, which throws away the sign of w☆, so +w☆ and −w☆ produce literally the same data. The loss has no way to tell them apart, so both valleys are equally good solutions.
  2. The origin is a hill, not a valley. A random starting guess lands somewhere near the origin (small in every coordinate). To get into one of the valleys, the ball has to skirt past one of the two saddles (the open circles sitting between the origin and the stars). That awkward detour is where the long plateau in §0’s curves comes from.
  3. Sliding a stretches the landscape along one direction, namely the high-variance direction of the data. The two valleys, the hill, and the two saddles all stay exactly where they are; what changes are the slopes between them. So anisotropy reshapes the path the ball takes, but it doesn’t change which valleys exist.

§2Two numbers, not d

In §1 we got away with two dimensions because the landscape was drawable, but real phase retrieval has 1000 (or more), and you can’t draw a 1000-dimensional surface. The good news is that you don’t have to. The loss only depends on two numbers about your current guess, so however many dimensions are involved, every training run is still just a path in 2D.

At each moment of training, those two summary numbers tell you where the guess is:

  • alignment (the paper’s u, defined as ⟨w, w☆⟩Q): how well the guess aligns with w☆. It starts near zero (a random vector in 1000 dimensions is essentially uncorrelated with w☆) and grows toward one as training succeeds. “Alignment” is our shorthand label, not the paper’s.
  • size (the paper’s s, defined as ⟨w, Qw⟩): how big the guess is, with high-variance directions counted more heavily. It starts near zero and grows toward one when training is done. “Size” is our shorthand label, not the paper’s.

That is it. Those two numbers are everything the loss ever sees. So no matter how big the dimension gets, training just traces a single curve in this 2D plane.

The five faded curves are the five training runs from §0, redrawn in this 2D plane. The black one is whatever a your slider is on. They all start near the origin (a random initial guess has tiny alignment and tiny size) and they all end at the star in the upper right, where alignment and size are both equal to one and training has converged.

The open circle sitting at (0, 1/3) is the same saddle you met in §1, just expressed in (alignment, size) coordinates instead of (w1, w2). Random init drops you near the lower-left corner, and the trajectory has to crawl up the size axis past that saddle before alignment is able to take off. That sluggish detour around the saddle is exactly the plateau you saw in §0’s curves.

There’s one thing this picture is hiding from you, though: how fast the dot is actually moving along that path. That’s what the next section gets into.

§3Three phases of training

What §2 was hiding is the timing. The dot moves along its path at a speed that varies enormously: it can crawl along for tens of thousands of steps and then cover most of the remaining distance in a few hundred. The cleanest way to see this is to just plot alignment, size, and MSE against time directly. When you do, three distinct phases jump out at you, separated by two flat shoulders.

  1. Phase I: escape from the saddle. Alignment crawls upward from a tiny initial value (random init gives you roughly 1/√d, where d is the number of dimensions). The dot in §2 was lingering near (0, 1/3) during this entire stretch, so on this plot the alignment line looks flat while the size line just drifts up to about 1/3 and stalls there.
  2. Phase II: rapid convergence. Once alignment finally escapes, both summary numbers sprint toward one in lockstep, and the MSE drops fast right along with them.
  3. Phase III: the spectral tail. Alignment and size are both pinned at one (the §2 dot is now sitting right on top of the star), and as far as the two summary numbers are concerned, training is finished. But the MSE in the bottom panel keeps falling, sometimes for orders of magnitude longer. Clearly something is still happening that those two summary numbers can’t see.

That last point is the loose thread, and the next section pulls on it.

§4The spectral tail

By the time those two summary numbers reach one, the guess has the right alignment and the right size on average. But the guess is really a vector with one entry per dimension, and on a coordinate-by-coordinate level there is plenty of work left to do. To see this directly, plot the squared error of every single coordinate at five different points in training:

  1. The leftmost coordinates correspond to the high-variance directions of the data, and they drop to near-zero error first. Each coordinate is learned at a rate set roughly by its variance, and at the head of the spectrum the variance is huge.
  2. The rightmost coordinates are the low-variance directions, and by exactly the same logic they decay slowly. They stay above zero for orders of magnitude longer than the head does.
  3. And here is the punchline: as you crank a up, the gap between the head and the tail variances grows. The head ends up learning faster and the tail learning slower at the same time. Two effects, one knob.

The MSE plotted in §0 is just the average of these per-coordinate squared errors. The MSE plateau during Phase III is the tail being slowly chipped away one coordinate at a time, with the slowest coordinates holding the average up.

So that’s half of §0’s puzzle answered: that’s why the tail is slow. What’s still missing is the other half, namely why anisotropy speeds up the early escape too. That’s the next section.

§5The trade-off, in one picture

This is where the two halves of the story come together. The single knob a pushes two different rates in opposite directions: as a grows, the rate at which alignment escapes from the saddle goes up, and the rate at which the tail of the spectrum gets learned goes down. Same parameter, two effects, working at cross purposes.

The escape rate (green) is how quickly alignment grows from near zero, just after random initialisation. As a increases, the data’s variance gets concentrated onto a single dominant direction, which means the gradient near the saddle ends up concentrated there too. Any random starting guess has at least some tiny component along that dominant direction, and that component grows fast. So the bigger a is, the faster you escape.

The tail rate (red-brown) is how quickly the slowest component of w☆ is learned. Each component decays at a rate set by its variance in the data, and once a gets large the lowest-variance component has almost no variance left at all. So the slowest component decays painfully slowly, and it’s what drags the MSE down through the entire spectral tail.

And that, finally, is the answer to §0. Five different values of a land on five different points along these two curves, and out come five different learning curves.

Behind the math

If §2 told you the loss only depends on two numbers, you might reasonably be wondering why the paper needs thirty pages of math. Here’s the catch: the loss only depends on (u, s), but the dynamics of (u, s) themselves do not.

Look at how the size s evolves. Differentiating the definition s = ∑i λi wi2 with respect to time gives an expression that involves ∑i λi2 wi2, which is a higher-order moment, call it s(2). To predict s, you need s(2). To predict s(2), you need s(3). And so on. There is no clean ODE for (u, s) alone.

Unless something special collapses the cascade. And in the isotropic case, something does: when all eigenvalues are equal, every higher moment is just the first one rescaled, so the whole tower collapses into a 2D ODE in (u, s). For anisotropic data the eigenvalues differ, the moments are independent, and the dynamics genuinely live in an infinite-dimensional space. The paper’s machinery (Duhamel’s formula, Volterra integral equations, phase-by-phase approximations) is the technology built to handle that.

You can see the closure break by plotting the moments s, s(2), s(3), s(4) over time, each divided by its own initial value (so they all start at 1). At a = 0 the four curves overlap exactly: knowing how one of them grows tells you how all the others grow. As a grows the curves peel apart, with each higher moment evolving on its own schedule.

Slide a from 0 upward. At a = 0 all four curves lie on top of each other and the closure holds. By a = 1 they’re clearly peeling apart, and by a = 2 they look like four genuinely different stories. That divergence is the whole reason this paper is hard.

§6The whole picture, all at once

After seeing each aspect of the trajectory in its own section, here they all are synchronised. The top slider sets the spectrum exponent a; the bottom one sets the current moment in training. Drag the time slider, and the dot in the (u, s) plane walks along the path while vertical lines on the time-series plots track the same moment. Drag a, and the whole thing reshapes.

Three things worth playing with:

  1. Hold a fixed and slide time. Watch the dot in (u, s) trace along the same path you saw in §2 while the vertical line in the time-series tracks where you are. The three phases come back as a feel: the dot lingers near the saddle, then sprints to the star, and then sits at (1, 1) while the MSE keeps falling.
  2. Slide a. Phase I shrinks, Phase III grows, and the (u, s) trajectory bends a bit. The per-coordinate error chart on the right reshapes its head and tail.
  3. Pick a fixed time, say t = 104, and slide a. Two trajectories at the exact same moment can be in completely different parts of training.

§7Why this matters

The paper isn’t really about phase retrieval, even though phase retrieval is the only thing it talks about. It’s about how the spectral structure of training data shapes the entire learning curve. Phase retrieval is just the cleanest nonlinear setting where you can do the math from end to end.

Real neural networks trained on real data follow power-law scaling laws: error drops as a power of compute, dataset size, or model size, often with exponents that stay remarkably consistent across many orders of magnitude. Where those exponents come from, and how they relate to the structure of the data, has been a long-standing question.

For linear models and kernel methods we’ve known the answer for years: the spectral decay of the kernel sets the exponents. What this paper does is push the same picture into a nonlinear, nonconvex setting with saddles and a sign-symmetric loss. And the trade-off you watched in §5 isn’t just a numerical observation. It falls out of the paper’s analysis as a closed-form result, with explicit scaling exponents in a.

Whether the same story carries over to deep neural networks is still open. But the shapes line up: long plateaus, sharp drops, slow power-law tails. The five training runs you saw at the top of the page are one rigorously understood instance of what scaling laws look like, and now you know exactly why they look that way.
