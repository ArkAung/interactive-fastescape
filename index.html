Fast Escape, Slow Convergence

Braun, Loureiro, Minh, Imaizumi · arXiv:2511.18661

If you’ve ever trained a neural network and stared at the loss curve, you’ve probably noticed it has a personality of its own. There’s a long flat stretch where almost nothing happens, then a sharp drop, then a slow tail that goes on practically forever. People have been watching curves of that shape for years now, and somewhere along the way they got a name: scaling laws. The natural question is where the shape actually comes from.

For linear models we know the answer pretty well by now. It comes down to the spectrum of the data’s covariance, which is basically a list of how much variance the data carries in each principal direction. That spectrum sets the rate at which the loss falls, and the curve drops out. For nonlinear models the story has been mostly empirical: people fit power laws and notice regularities, but proper derivations have been hard to come by. This page is a walkthrough of a recent paper that nails down a scaling law in the cleanest nonlinear setting we’ve got, a problem called phase retrieval.

Here’s the setup. There is some unknown direction w☆ hiding in a high-dimensional space, and your job is to find it. You don’t get to look at it. What you get to do is fire random vectors x at it and observe y = (x · w☆)2, plus a little noise. That squaring is the whole reason the problem is interesting. It throws away the sign of w☆: flipping the answer to −w☆ gives back exactly the same observations, so the loss ends up with two equally valid minima and a path between them that is anything but trivial.

One single knob, a, controls everything. It says how the input variance is spread across directions. At a = 0 every direction has the same variance and the data is a uniform fuzzy ball. As a grows, more and more of the variance gets squeezed into a few dominant directions, until at large a the data is basically a thin needle along one axis. The five training runs in §0 differ only in this one knob, and as you’ll see, that’s enough to change the learning curve completely. The rest of the page is a walk through why: the loss landscape (§1), the two summary numbers that describe a trajectory (§2), the three phases of training (§3), the slow spectral tail (§4), and finally the trade-off (§5) that resolves the §0 puzzle.

§0Five training runs

Five training runs on the same problem. The five plots below were all produced by the same gradient flow on the same loss with the same amount of noise. The only thing that changes between them is one parameter, a, and yet the curves come out wildly different. Working out why is what the rest of this page is about.

So what is a doing? Picture the training inputs as a cloud of points scattered through a high-dimensional space. The knob a controls how that cloud is shaped. At a = 0 it is a uniform fuzzy ball, with every direction having the same spread. Turn a up and the cloud starts getting stretched along one axis and squashed along the rest, until at large a almost all of the variation in the data lives in just a handful of dominant directions. §1What gradient flow has to climb

Training is just an optimization problem. There’s a loss function L(w), and you want a setting of w that makes it as small as you can get it. The way to picture this is to imagine the loss as a hilly landscape: every possible w is a position on the ground, and the height there is the loss value. Training rolls a ball downhill until it stops at a minimum. We can’t draw the landscape for 1000-dimensional phase retrieval, but we can draw it in 2D. Click anywhere on the picture below to drop a ball and watch where it ends up.

A few things are worth pointing out about this picture:

  1. There are two valleys, not one. Both stars are equally deep, and they sit at exactly opposite directions. Phase retrieval squares an inner product, which throws away the sign of the answer, so +w☆ and −w☆ produce literally the same data. The loss has no way to tell them apart, so both valleys are equally good answers.
  2. The origin is a hill, not a valley. A random starting guess lands somewhere near the origin (small in every coordinate). To get into one of the valleys, the ball has to skirt past one of the two saddles (the open circles sitting between the origin and the stars). That awkward detour is where the long plateau in §0’s curves comes from.
  3. Sliding a stretches the landscape along one direction, namely the high-variance direction of the data. The two valleys, the hill, and the two saddles all stay exactly where they are; what changes are the slopes between them. So anisotropy reshapes the path the ball takes, but it doesn’t change which valleys exist.

§2Two numbers, not d

In §1 we got away with two dimensions because the landscape was drawable, but real phase retrieval has 1000 (or more), and you can’t draw a 1000-dimensional surface. The good news is that you don’t have to. The loss only depends on two numbers about your current guess, so however many dimensions are involved, every training run is still just a path in 2D.

At each moment of training, those two summary numbers tell you where the guess is:

  • alignment: how well the guess points along the hidden answer. It starts near zero (a random direction is essentially uncorrelated with the answer) and grows toward one as training succeeds. Plotted as u.
  • size: how big the guess is, with high-variance directions counted more heavily. It starts near zero (a random unit vector has tiny weighted norm in 1000 dimensions) and grows toward one when training is done. Plotted as s.

That is it. Those two numbers are everything the loss ever sees. So no matter how big the dimension gets, training just traces a single curve in this 2D plane.

The five faded curves are the five training runs from §0, redrawn in this 2D plane. The black one is whatever a your slider is on. They all start near the origin (a random initial guess has tiny alignment and tiny size) and they all end at the star in the upper right, where alignment and size are both equal to one and training has converged.

The open circle sitting at (0, 1/3) is the same saddle you met in §1, just expressed in (alignment, size) coordinates instead of (w1, w2). Random init drops you near the lower-left corner, and the trajectory has to crawl up the size axis past that saddle before alignment is able to take off. That sluggish detour around the saddle is exactly the plateau you saw in §0’s curves.

There’s one thing this picture is hiding from you, though: how fast the dot is actually moving along that path. That’s what the next section gets into.

§3Three phases of training

Plot the alignment (u), size (s), and MSE against time. Three distinct phases pop out, separated by two flat shoulders.

  1. Phase I: escape from the saddle. The alignment crawls upward from a tiny initial value (random init gives roughly 1/√d for d-dimensional space). The dot in §2 was lingering near (0, 1/3); on this plot the alignment line looks flat while the size line nudges up to about 1/3.
  2. Phase II: convergence. Once alignment escapes, both summary numbers sprint toward 1. The MSE drops fast.
  3. Phase III: the spectral tail. Alignment and size both pin at 1 (the §2 dot is sitting at the star). But the MSE in the bottom panel keeps falling, sometimes for orders of magnitude. Something is still happening that the two summary numbers don’t capture.

That last point is what the next section is about.

§4The spectral tail

After alignment and size both reach 1, the guess has the right size and the right alignment on average. But the guess has many components (one per dimension), and component-by-component, plenty of error remains. Plot the squared error of every component at five snapshots in time:

  1. The leftmost components (the high-variance directions of the data) drop to near-zero error first. They’re learned at a rate set by their variance, which is large at the head of the spectrum.
  2. The rightmost components (the low-variance directions) decay at a rate set by their tiny variance. They stay above zero for orders of magnitude longer.
  3. For larger a, the gap between the largest and smallest variances grows. So the head learns faster, and the tail learns slower. Two effects, one parameter.

The MSE you saw in §0 is just the average of these per-component errors. The plateau in MSE during Phase III is the tail being slowly chipped away.

Now we have half of §0's puzzle: why slow tail. The other half is why fast escape. That's next.

§5The trade-off, in one picture

Anisotropy is a single knob that pushes two rates in opposite directions. The escape rate from the saddle goes up; the tail-learning rate goes down. Same a, both effects.

Escape rate (green): how fast alignment grows from near zero. As a increases, the data’s variance concentrates onto a single dominant direction, which means the gradient near the saddle is concentrated there too. A random starting guess always has SOME component in that direction, and that component grows fast. Escape time shrinks.

Tail rate (red-brown): how fast the slowest component of the answer is learned. Each component is learned at a rate set by its variance in the data. As a grows, the lowest-variance component has almost no variance left, so it learns painfully slowly. That slow component holds the MSE up.

This is the answer to §0. Five values of a, five trade-offs along these two curves, five different learning curves.

§6Why this matters

The paper isn’t really about phase retrieval. It’s about how the spectral structure of the data shapes a learning curve. Phase retrieval is the simplest nonlinear setting where the math is tractable end-to-end.

Neural networks trained on real data follow power-law scaling: error decays as a power of compute, dataset size, or model size, often consistent across orders of magnitude. Why those exponents, and how they relate to the data, has been a long-standing question.

For linear models and kernel methods, the picture has been understood for years: the spectral decay of the kernel sets the scaling exponents. What this paper does is extend the picture to a nonlinear, nonconvex setting with saddles and a sign-symmetric loss. The same trade-off you watched in §5 falls out of the analysis as a closed-form result, with explicit scaling exponents in a.

Whether the same story holds for deep networks is open. But the shapes of the curves match the empirical phenomenology: long plateaus, sharp drops, slow power-law tails. The five training runs in §0 are one instance of what scaling laws look like, and now you know exactly why they look like that.
