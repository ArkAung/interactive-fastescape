Fast Escape, Slow Convergence

Braun, Loureiro, Minh, Imaizumi · arXiv:2511.18661

§0Five training runs

Five gradient flows on the same phase retrieval loss. They differ only in the data’s covariance spectrum: λi ∝ i−a, for five values of a.

The spectrum is the variance of x along each principal axis: λ1 the largest, λd the smallest. A flat spectrum (a = 0) means equal variance in every direction. A steep one (large a) means most of the variance is concentrated in a few directions.

Slide a. The spectrum steepens, the data cloud flattens, and the learning curve drops earlier but plateaus longer.

§1The loss

Each observation is y = ⟨x, w☆⟩2 + ξ. Squaring the inner product throws away the sign of w☆, so +w☆ and −w☆ produce identical data. The loss inherits that symmetry: it has two equally good minima.

To see this concretely, walk along the line w = αw☆ and watch the loss. It works out to

L(αw☆) = 3 s☆2 (α2 − 1)2,

a quartic in α with zeros at α = +1 and α = −1.

Two equal-depth wells at α = ±1. A bump at α = 0, which is where random initialization tends to start. The dashed line confirms that +α and −α always have the same loss.

That's a 1D slice. In two dimensions you get the same wells plus extra structure: saddles sitting between the two minima.

Stars: the two global minima at ±w☆. White dot: the strict local maximum at the origin. Open circles: the two saddles. Sliding a stretches Q (the data covariance), which warps the landscape but leaves all five critical points intact. To go from the origin to either minimum, gradient flow has to navigate around a saddle, and that's where the long plateau in §0 comes from.
